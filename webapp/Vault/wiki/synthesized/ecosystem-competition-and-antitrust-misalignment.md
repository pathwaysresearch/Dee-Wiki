# Ecosystem Competition and the Structural Misalignment of Antitrust Policy

## The Core Misalignment

Venkatraman's *Digital Matrix* establishes that the fundamental unit of competition in the digital economy is not the individual firm but the **ecosystem**. Incumbent companies, digital giants, and tech entrepreneurs compete as assembled constellations of capabilities, data flows, and partner networks. This architectural shift creates a structural misalignment with traditional antitrust policy, which was designed to measure the market power of single firms within defined sector boundaries.

If competitive advantage is ecosystem-level rather than firm-level, then antitrust metrics anchored to single-firm market share, pricing power, or vertical integration within a single industry systematically undercount and misattribute platform dominance. The monopoly power at issue is not the power to set prices in one market—it is the power to define the rules of participation across multiple interlocking markets simultaneously.

## The Predix Case as Illustration

GE's Predix platform demonstrates how an analytics offering becomes a **cross-industry infrastructure layer**. By coupling industrial assets (turbines, locomotives, medical equipment) with real-time sensor data and cloud-based analytics, Predix generates data-driven network effects that do not respect sector lines: data from aviation maintenance improves predictive models for energy, and vice versa. The platform becomes more valuable to each vertical as participation in other verticals grows.

This is the precise structure that traditional sector-specific regulation cannot address. Aviation regulators, energy regulators, and healthcare regulators each see a portion of the platform's footprint—but none sees the cross-industry data accumulation that constitutes the actual competitive moat. The regulatory picture is systematically fragmented while the platform's advantage is structurally integrated.

## From Business Strategy to Policy Analog

Venkatraman's strategic principle—*know when to link and when to pull back*—has a direct public-policy analog. The question for governments is whether to:

1. **Couple regulation tightly to specific ecosystems**, appointing sector-specific overseers for each dominant platform (the traditional approach, extended).
2. **Establish horizontal principles of contestability**, defining platform-neutral obligations around data portability, interoperability, and algorithmic transparency that apply regardless of sector.

The EU's Digital Markets Act 'gatekeeper' concept is the most developed instantiation of option 2. By designating certain platforms as gatekeepers based on structural characteristics (user reach, data holdings, cross-market presence) rather than sector membership, it attempts to regulate at the ecosystem level rather than the firm-within-sector level. This is regulation designed for an ecosystem-competition world.

## Labor Share, Productivity, and the Ecosystem Lens

The ecosystem-as-unit-of-competition framing also reframes debates about labor share of income and job polarization. When productivity gains from AI-enabled automation and data network effects accrue primarily to ecosystem orchestrators (who capture value from all participants) rather than to complementors or workers, the distributional question becomes: **who owns the infrastructure layer?**

- Firms participating in a dominant ecosystem as complementors occupy what Venkatraman calls a structurally weak position—high dependence, limited exit options.
- Workers whose tasks are automated within an ecosystem face a double displacement: job polarization from automation *and* weakened bargaining power because the ecosystem orchestrator captures surplus that previously would have been distributed across a supply chain.

This suggests that labor-share erosion in high-IT-intensive industries may be partly a function of ecosystem concentration, not merely firm-level automation decisions—a distributional mechanism invisible to single-firm antitrust analysis.

## Reusable Framing: Regulatory Granularity vs. Ecosystem Granularity

A general principle emerges: **regulatory granularity should match competitive granularity**. When competition was firm-level and sector-bounded, sector-specific firm-level regulation was appropriately calibrated. When competition is ecosystem-level and cross-sector, regulation must operate at a correspondingly horizontal and structural level.

This is not an argument against regulation—it is an argument for regulatory architecture that is fit for purpose. The policy design question becomes less 'should governments intervene?' and more 'at what level of aggregation should intervention be targeted, and what are the tradeoffs between precision (ecosystem-specific rules) and generalizability (horizontal contestability principles)?'

## Key Tensions

| Dimension | Traditional Antitrust | Ecosystem-Level Regulation |
|---|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Single firm | Ecosystem / platform |
| Market definition | Sector-bounded | Cross-industry |
| Power metric | Market share | Data accumulation + interoperability leverage |
| Remedy orientation | Structural breakup / pricing | Contestability, portability, API access |
| Risk of over-intervention | Chilling firm-level innovation | Fragmenting beneficial cross-industry data networks |
| Risk of under-intervention | Missing ecosystem dominance | Entrenching infrastructure-layer monopolies |
