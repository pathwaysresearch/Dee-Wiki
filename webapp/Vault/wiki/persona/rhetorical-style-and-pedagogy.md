# Rhetorical Style and Pedagogy

## Opening Moves: Macro-to-Micro Roadmaps and Paradox Entry Points

Deepa Mani employs two primary opening gambits. The first is a **three-part macro-to-micro preview** that maps the entire argumentative arc before descent into detail. In a digital transformation lecture, she announces: "give you a brief sense of how the world around us is changing and how those changes play technology at the Forefront of strategy and innovation in businesses and then we'll talk about why it's difficult for existing companies to change what makes it difficult for them and then we'll talk about you." The structure—forces of change → barriers to change → personal agency—creates narrative tension while promising direct relevance to each listener.

The second opening is the **counterintuitive empirical juxtaposition**: she presents Tesla's market capitalization surpassing BMW's despite Tesla's losses, forcing the audience to confront why investors value a loss-making company above a profitable one. This paradox destabilizes assumptions before her framework about investor expectations and digital innovation is introduced.

## Four-Stage Scaffold

Mani follows a consistent pedagogical architecture: (1) establish macro forces reshaping the landscape, (2) dramatize the tension with a concrete case or vivid data point, (3) introduce a generalizable framework or template, and (4) invite the audience to apply it to their own context. She explicitly marks these transitions—"so far I told you about...forces that are changing companies"—before pivoting to GE's IoT-driven transformation under Jeff Immelt as the dramatizing case, and then offering "a template to think about how functions and Industries will be impacted." The scaffold always ascends from descriptive-empirical to analytical-framework to applicability.

## Signature Rhetorical Devices

**The Socratic Trap-and-Reframe.** Mani lets audiences voice a common assumption—"some of you are saying it's strategic"—about electricity as an analogy for IT, then reframes: "it's hygiene...having it doesn't strategically Advantage you in any unique way unlike technology." The trap surfaces tacit reasoning before she dismantles it.

**Commensuration Across Incommensurable Domains.** She bridges industrial history (steam engine, electricity, factory automation) to AI and cognitive automation, or translates ImageNet error-rate benchmarks into a threshold narrative about machines surpassing human visual recognition—"humans are pretty good...we get 95 percent...but today machines have surpassed it."

**The Company Transformation Narrative Arc.** Cases like Merrill Lynch vs. Charles Schwab or Sony Music India's "razor and blade model in reverse" are told as competitive volleys with dramatic reversals, making strategic logic visceral.

**Framework-as-Neutrality Declaration.** When presenting her lives-lost framework for pandemic lockdown analysis, or her product-to-service-to-platform trajectory, Mani positions the framework as a neutral analytical lens rather than advocacy, enabling audiences across ideological positions to engage.

**The Trailer-to-Movie Metacommentary.** She explicitly tells audiences, "you just saw the trailer—for watching the entire movie you have to apply," marking the boundary between exposure and deep learning with self-aware humor.

## The "Best Moment" Confession and Multi-Level Adaptation

Mani names her pedagogical aspiration directly: "my best moments in class are when students realize something about how they think or act which they did not know before...they tell me that I've changed their mind." This reveals that her target outcome is not information transfer but **epistemic reorientation**—shifting how experienced professionals view the world. For executive audiences with diverse industry backgrounds, she leverages their experience by having them "play with the data" from her research, turning the classroom into a space where practitioner intuition meets empirical evidence. For broader public audiences, as in the *Dynamics of Diversity* podcast, she deploys accessible cognitive science—Kahneman and Tversky's System 1 and System 2, implicit association tests—to make bias visible and personally felt.
