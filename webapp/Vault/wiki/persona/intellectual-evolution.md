# Intellectual Evolution

## Phase 1: Education at Elite Institutions (1990–2005)

Deepa Mani's formative intellectual orientation was shaped by the Indian IT industry's own trajectory. During this period, the dominant narrative was India as a low-cost provider of transaction-intensive services. Her doctoral work, influenced by advisors like Anandhi Bharadwaj and Kannan Srikanth, anchored her in the information systems discipline but with an early instinct to study organizational transitions rather than technology artifacts in isolation. The key seed planted here was the question of how firms move "from the periphery to the core" — a phrase she later attributed to Howard — which became a through-line across her career. She had not yet developed her signature concern with governance mechanisms, but the groundwork was laid in understanding capability transitions within the Indian IT outsourcing sector.

## Phase 2: Early Faculty and Research Leadership (2005–2016)

The shift in this phase was from studying IT service providers abstractly to anchoring research in rich Indian institutional cases. Mani developed the **IT-Led Transformation at Reliance Energy** and **Sony Music India** case studies, moving from pure outsourcing governance toward a broader concern with how technology disrupts business models. The Sony Music case crystallized her insight that incumbents who cede innovation to platform providers ("they would never innovate ever again in the history of music") face existential strategic loss. Simultaneously, her research on **Common Service Centres (CSCs)** — studying village-level entrepreneurs' marketing strategies and G2C-to-B2C expansion — marked the addition of bottom-of-pyramid technology entrepreneurship to her portfolio. She abandoned a narrow focus on multinational captive centers and added a commitment to contextual translation of Western IS frameworks to Indian conditions. The trigger was direct fieldwork revealing that technology adoption patterns in rural India operated under entirely different governance and resource constraints than corporate settings.

## Phase 3: Administrative Leadership and Policy Engagement (2016–2023)

Two external shocks catalyzed dramatic intellectual expansion. First, the **COVID-19 pandemic** pushed Mani into public policy analysis, producing work on a "lives-lost framework" comparing pandemic mortality against economic contraction deaths — a commensuration across domains (macroeconomics and public health) that was new territory for an IS scholar. She explicitly framed this as providing "a framework for evaluating this decision" rather than advocating a position, reflecting her signature framework-as-neutrality stance. Second, the remote work upheaval led to her research on **Need for Human Presence (NHP)** and productivity differentials in remote settings, grounded in a survey of 500–700 IT workers. She also launched the **Dynamics of Diversity** podcast, integrating Kahneman and Tversky's dual-process theory and implicit association test research into arguments about gender bias in organizations. What she abandoned in this phase was the assumption that her research audience was primarily academic; she began designing executive education programs and policy briefs with practitioner applicability as a first-order concern.

## Phase 4: Senior Academic Leadership (2023–2026)

Mani's most recent evolution integrates AI capability thresholds — citing ImageNet benchmarks where machine vision surpassed human 95% accuracy — into her long-standing industrial history analogies (steam engine to electric motor to cognitive automation). The Tesla-vs-BMW market capitalization paradox became a signature teaching device, encoding her mature theoretical position: investor expectations constrain digital innovation trajectories in ways that separate high-growth from high-profit firms. She now operates at the intersection of technology strategy, organizational behavior, and policy, having moved from studying how firms adopt technology to studying how entire ecosystems — investors, regulators, entrepreneurs, and cognitive biases — co-determine technology's societal impact. Her "best moments in class" confession — "when students realize something about how they think or act which they did not know before" — signals that the ultimate object of transformation in her intellectual arc has shifted from organizations to minds.
