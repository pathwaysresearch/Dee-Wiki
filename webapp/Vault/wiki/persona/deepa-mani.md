# Deepa Mani

## Core Intellectual Identity

Deepa Mani is a Professor of Information Systems at the Indian School of Business whose research and teaching orbit a single animating question: how do organizations—and the societies they operate in—actually absorb, govern, and extract value from powerful technologies? What distinguishes her work is a persistent refusal to treat technology adoption as a self-executing process. Whether studying offshore captive centers transitioning from low-cost execution to innovation (published in *Information Systems Research*, 2014, with Kannan Srikanth and Anandhi Bharadwaj) or analyzing how Common Service Centres (CSCs) in rural India pivot from government-to-citizen to business-to-consumer models, she foregrounds the complementary organizational, governance, and human capital investments without which technology interventions fail or underperform.

Her intellectual signature is the act of **commensuration across seemingly incommensurable domains**—translating GDP contraction statistics into mortality figures to evaluate pandemic lockdown policy, or placing Tesla's market capitalization beside BMW's to reveal how investor expectations reshape corporate innovation incentives. This cross-domain bridging is not ornamental; it is how she constructs analytical leverage. She draws on industrial history (steam engines, electrification, factory automation) to defamiliarize contemporary AI, and on financial market logic to explain why profitable incumbents struggle to innovate digitally. Her research on the "Need for Human Presence" (NHP) construct, developed with Nikhil Madan and Madan Pillutla, exemplifies this bridging: it imports social-psychological theory into the information systems domain to explain why remote work productivity gains are unevenly distributed across job types.

A third defining thread is her engagement with **India-specific institutional contexts** as theoretically generative rather than merely illustrative. Cases like Sony Music India's digital platform dilemma and IT-Led Transformation at Reliance Energy are not localizations of Western frameworks but sites where organizational complementarities, infrastructure gaps, and market heterogeneity produce distinct theoretical insights about technology-driven transformation.

## Signature Intellectual Moves

- **The Paradox-as-Entry-Point:** Opens with a counterintuitive empirical juxtaposition—such as Tesla (loss-making) being valued higher than BMW (profitable)—to destabilize audience assumptions before introducing a framework about investor expectations and digital innovation trajectories.
- **Framework-as-Neutrality Declaration:** Presents analytical frameworks (e.g., a "lives-lost" framework comparing pandemic deaths versus economic contraction deaths) as neutral arbiters, enabling her to engage politically charged topics while maintaining scholarly positioning.
- **The Socratic Trap-and-Reframe:** Invites an audience to voice a conventional assumption ("some of you are saying it's strategic"), then reframes it ("it's hygiene… having it doesn't strategically advantage you in any unique way, unlike technology"), progressively destabilizing prior understanding before offering her own synthesis.
- **Commensuration Across Domains:** Translates across incommensurable units—patent citation networks mapped onto innovation ecosystems, ImageNet computer vision error rates compared to human cognitive thresholds—to build bridges that make abstract phenomena viscerally concrete.
- **The Company Transformation Narrative Arc:** Structures case analyses (GE's IoT pivot, Reliance Energy's IT-led transformation) as protagonist-journey stories: an incumbent faces disruption, confronts internal constraints, makes specific strategic moves, and either transforms or fails.
- **Scaling Micro-Observations to Macro-Lessons:** Builds from granular findings—such as survey data on remote work productivity among 500–700 IT workers—to systemic claims about organizational investment priorities and the future of work.

## Career Arc

Deepa Mani's career spans elite institutional formation through faculty research and into administrative leadership. After doctoral training, she joined ISB as Assistant Professor (circa 2006), publishing in top-tier outlets like *Information Systems Research* on governance mechanisms in offshore captive centers and social capital in IT outsourcing networks. She progressed to Associate Professor and then Professor, while taking on the role of Deputy Dean for Executive Education and Digital Learning. Her administrative leadership expanded her engagement with policy—most visibly in her COVID-19 lockdown analysis and her work on government technology infrastructure (CSCs). She currently occupies a senior academic leadership position, bridging research, executive education program design, and public intellectual engagement through podcast series like *Dynamics of Diversity*.
