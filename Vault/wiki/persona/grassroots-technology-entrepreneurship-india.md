# Grassroots Technology Entrepreneurship in India

## Distinctive Take

Deepa Mani's view of grassroots technology entrepreneurship departs from the typical "digital inclusion" narrative by treating village-level entrepreneurs not as passive beneficiaries of government infrastructure but as strategic agents making genuine business decisions under constraint. Her research on Common Service Centres (CSCs) reframes what could be read as a public utility story into an entrepreneurship story—one in which rural operators navigate marketing strategies, capital sourcing, and service portfolio expansion just as any firm would. The non-obvious move is treating the CSC scheme as a lens on how government-to-citizen (G2C) platforms can organically evolve into business-to-consumer (B2C) ventures, making the state an inadvertent incubator of bottom-of-pyramid entrepreneurship at massive scale.

## Key Frameworks

Mani applies a **G2C-to-B2C service expansion model** to explain how CSCs, originally designed as delivery points for government services—public utility, social welfare, healthcare, financial, education, and agriculture services—became platforms for a "whole range of B2C products and services." This trajectory mirrors her broader framework of platform evolution, where initial infrastructure provision creates network effects that enable entrepreneurial value capture. She also draws on her signature **scaling micro-observations to macro-lessons** pattern: individual entrepreneur strategies (word-of-mouth marketing, family-funded capital, local trust networks) are aggregated into system-level insights about what makes grassroots digital ecosystems sustainable.

## Concrete Cases and Evidence

The CSC scheme is the anchor case. Mani highlights that there are "more than eight and a half lakh CSCs today," with "several hundreds of these earning well over 100 crores in annual revenue"—figures she uses to establish that the phenomenon is not marginal but structurally significant. She documents the heterogeneity of entrepreneur strategies within this system: some employ deliberate marketing strategies to expand beyond their immediate community, while others adopt word-of-mouth approaches "restricting their business only to the local villagers or a population that they knew." Some rely on family funds, constraining investment but reducing risk. Each centre is "run and managed by a local entrepreneur who owns, operates, and maintains the computer and the associated equipment" and shares revenue with the government, "thereby creating a sustainable ecosystem." Mani uses this variation to argue that entrepreneurial agency—not just infrastructure provision—determines outcomes.

## Connection to the Broader Intellectual Project

This domain sits at the intersection of several of Mani's core themes. The CSC research is a natural extension of her work on **technology-driven transformation in Indian industry contexts** (Sony Music India, Reliance Energy), scaled down from large corporations to village-level operators. It connects to her interest in **governance mechanisms and organizational complementarities**: the CSC ecosystem requires complementary organizational change (revenue-sharing models, digital literacy, local trust) alongside technology deployment. It also feeds her **policy frameworks** stream—the CSC scheme is government policy, and understanding its entrepreneurial dynamics informs whether such schemes should be evaluated as welfare programs or as economic development engines. The phrase she uses—"entrepreneurship very aspirational in the rural areas in which the scheme is embedded"—reveals her consistent concern with how technology reshapes not just operations but identities and ambitions. This is the same intellectual move she makes when discussing how Uber redefines what it means to be in the "automobile" business, but applied to the bottom of the pyramid rather than Silicon Valley.
