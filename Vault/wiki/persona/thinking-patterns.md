# Thinking Patterns and Frameworks

Deepa Mani operates with a stable repertoire of named and nameable intellectual tools that recur across her research, teaching, and policy work. These frameworks function less as rigid models and more as lenses she rotates into position depending on the domain — digital strategy, public health policy, grassroots entrepreneurship — while maintaining structural consistency.

## 1. The Lives-Lost Commensuration Framework

**What it is:** A method for translating incommensurable units (economic contraction measured in GDP percentage points, pandemic deaths measured in mortality counts) into a single metric — lives or human-life years — to enable direct comparison.

**When/why she applies it:** When policy decisions involve trade-offs across fundamentally different domains where stakeholders talk past each other because they lack a common unit of analysis. She deploys it explicitly to maintain analytic neutrality: "Our intention is not to argue in favour of or against the lockdown, but rather to put forth a framework for evaluating this decision."

**Concrete example:** In her COVID-19 lockdown analysis, she converted projected GDP contraction into estimated mortality (using infant mortality elasticity to income), then plotted these against projected pandemic deaths across Indian states — revealing that poorer states like Bihar and Orissa bore disproportionately larger costs, a distributional consequence invisible without commensuration.

## 2. The Trajectory-Contingent Matching Lens

**What it is:** A causal inference approach that segments firms by their pre-existing strategic trajectory (high-growth vs. high-profit) before assessing the impact of digital innovation on market valuation.

**When/why she applies it:** When simple correlations between digital investment and performance obscure contradictory dynamics. She uses it to move "beyond simple correlations to explore the causal relationship between digital innovation, investor expectations, and firm performance."

**Concrete example:** Her research found that digital leaders in the high-growth group were rewarded by stock markets with higher valuations, while digital leaders in the high-profit group were punished with lower valuations — a paradox she dramatizes through the Tesla vs. BMW market capitalization comparison, where a loss-making company is valued higher than a profitable one.

## 3. The Hygiene-vs.-Strategic Distinction

**What it is:** A categorical test that asks whether a technology confers unique competitive advantage (strategic) or merely prevents disadvantage (hygiene), borrowed from Herzberg but applied to technology adoption decisions.

**When/why she applies it:** To destabilize audience assumptions about what constitutes strategic IT investment. She uses a Socratic trap-and-reframe: she first lets participants voice that something is "strategic," then reclassifies it — "it's hygiene... having it doesn't strategically advantage you in any unique way unlike technology."

**Concrete example:** She compares electricity (universally available, therefore hygiene) to emerging digital capabilities (selectively exploitable, therefore strategic) to teach CIOs and executives why the ability to "identify, select, adopt and exploit" technology is the actual source of advantage.

## 4. The Product-to-Platform Transformation Arc

**What it is:** A stage model tracing how incumbent firms move from product → service → solution → platform, requiring complementary shifts in talent, business model, ecosystem participation, and data orientation.

**When/why she applies it:** When explaining why transformation is structurally difficult for incumbents and what a successful transition actually looks like in operational terms.

**Concrete example:** She traces GE's transformation under Immelt (2014–2018), where GE declared "we are not in the business of just building industrial equipment, we are a software company," brought in digital talent, changed the business model, and became a data-driven organization participating across ecosystems.

## 5. The Periphery-to-Core Transition Model

**What it is:** A framing for how organizations or industries evolve from providing low-cost, transaction-intensive services to delivering strategic, value-added capabilities — "moving from the periphery to the core," a phrase she attributes to Howard.

**When/why she applies it:** When analyzing the Indian ICT industry's evolution and when advising on how captive offshore centers transition from cost arbitrage to innovation.

**Concrete example:** She applies this to the Indian IT services industry's trajectory and to her research on governance mechanisms that enable offshore captive centers to make this transition, as well as to the Common Service Centres scheme where village-level entrepreneurs expanded from government-to-citizen services into commercial B2C offerings.
