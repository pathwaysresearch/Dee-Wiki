# Mentor Network and Influences

## Anandhi Bharadwaj — Governance and the Offshore Innovation Transition

Deepa Mani's most sustained intellectual partnership is with Anandhi Bharadwaj, with whom she co-authored "Efficacy of R&D Work in Offshore Captive Centers: An Empirical Study of Task Characteristics, Coordination Mechanisms and Performance" (*Information Systems Research*, 2014). This collaboration anchors Mani's core research agenda on how offshore captive centers transition from low-cost service delivery to genuine innovation. Bharadwaj's influence is visible in Mani's persistent attention to governance mechanisms and organizational complementarities — the idea that technology adoption succeeds only when paired with deliberate investments in coordination structures and capability building.

## Kannan Srikanth — Coordination Under Organizational Boundaries

Kannan Srikanth, listed alongside Bharadwaj on the captive centers research, shapes Mani's thinking about how task characteristics and coordination mechanisms interact across organizational boundaries. His influence reinforces Mani's tendency to decompose complex organizational phenomena into constituent governance and coordination variables rather than treating transformation as monolithic.

## Madan Pillutla — Behavioral Science and the Future of Work

The collaboration with Madan Pillutla (London Business School) on the "Need for Human Presence" (NHP) construct and remote work productivity represents Mani's bridge into behavioral and organizational psychology. Their joint work, including the *Hindu Business Line* article "Does work-from-home work for all?", directly informs Mani's framework for understanding how cognitive and interpersonal job characteristics mediate technology's impact on productivity — a theme she extends into her Dynamics of Diversity podcast series.

## Bhagwan Chowdhry — Economics, Crisis, and Commensuration

Bhagwan Chowdhry's influence surfaces in Mani's distinctive rhetorical move of commensuration — translating across incommensurable units such as GDP contraction deaths versus pandemic deaths. This cross-domain bridging between macroeconomics and public health policy, central to her COVID-19 lockdown analysis, bears the imprint of Chowdhry's approach to quantifying trade-offs in policy contexts.

## Joseph Henrich — Cultural Evolution and Cognitive Biases

Mani draws on Joseph Henrich's work on cultural evolution to inform her treatment of cognitive biases and diversity. In her Dynamics of Diversity episodes, she invokes Kahneman and Tversky's System 1/System 2 framework alongside research on implicit association tests and stereotype confirmation bias, but Henrich's broader cultural lens shapes her insistence that biases are not merely individual failures but institutionally embedded patterns requiring organizational-level intervention.

## Dialogical Settings

Mani develops ideas in three primary settings: co-authored empirical research with IS scholars (Bharadwaj, Srikanth, Anjana Susarla, Vijay Gurbaxani on the social capital and IT outsourcing paper in *ISR*, 2015); executive education programs where real-time corporate dialogue — such as Novartis participants describing their uneasy Google Life Sciences alliance — stress-tests her frameworks; and podcast-format public intellectualism where she translates academic findings into accessible narratives.
