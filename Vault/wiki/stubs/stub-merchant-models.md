# Merchant Models

**Type**: RAG stub — full content in vector index, not in wiki

## Abstract

This source is a lecture session (FRNT Session 9 by Vishal K) covering a framework for understanding retail, merchant, and eCommerce business models. It analyzes the advantages and disadvantages of eCommerce for consumers, examines potential sources of competitive advantage in internet markets—including pricing dynamics, price dispersion, and the Long Tail concept—and uses case studies such as Myntra, Amazon, and Barnes & Noble to illustrate key principles. The material explores how internet retailers differ from physical retailers in pricing behavior, inventory management, and selection breadth.

## Key Claims

- Retail strategy involves trade-offs among three interconnected elements: low prices, customer experience, and large selection
- Internet retailers exhibit greater price dispersion compared to physical retailers for products like books, with wider and flatter distribution curves
- Physical retailers show more frequent small price changes (concentrated around 0.05 increments) while internet pricing dynamics differ
- The Long Tail concept explains how digital and online retailers (like iTunes and Amazon) can profitably carry far more titles than physical retailers due to near-zero marginal costs for additional inventory
- Amazon's inventory efficiency (holding inventory equal to ~9.8% of revenues) represents a competitive advantage, and applying similar efficiency to Barnes & Noble would yield significant annual savings

eCommerce business models, retail strategy triangle (price/experience/selection), price dispersion in internet vs. physical markets, price changes comparison (online vs. offline), Long Tail economics, Amazon fulfillment and inventory management, Barnes & Noble competitive analysis, Myntra case study, competitive advantage in internet markets, digital goods distribution, consumer advantages/disadvantages of eCommerce, efficient inventory management
