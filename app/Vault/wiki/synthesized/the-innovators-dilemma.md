# The Innovator's Dilemma

# The Innovator's Dilemma

A phenomenon where incumbent companies fail to respond to disruptive threats because they are chasing higher profits and measure the disruptor's performance through their own lens.

## Core Reframe: Success as the Binding Constraint

The deeper insight behind the Innovator's Dilemma is not simply that incumbents are complacent — it is that **success itself becomes the binding constraint**. Good management practice, optimized for current performance, is the precise mechanism that blinds incumbents to disruptive threats.

This reframe matters because it removes the intuitive escape route: incumbents are not failing due to bad leadership or laziness. They are failing *because* they are doing everything right by conventional standards. They are:

- Listening to their best customers
- Investing in the highest-margin opportunities
- Improving products along trajectories customers currently value
- Allocating resources rationally based on existing profit pools

Each of these behaviors is a textbook best practice — and each one systematically filters out signals from disruptive entrants who initially serve marginal customers with inferior but cheaper or simpler products.

## The Paradox

The paradox is that the better a company executes on its current model, the more optimized it becomes for that model, and the less able it is to perceive or respond to a fundamentally different competitive logic. Success deepens the grooves of existing processes, values, and resource allocation rules — making deviation increasingly costly and culturally difficult.

This is why disruptive threats feel like non-threats for so long: they genuinely are non-threats *within the incumbent's frame of reference*. The disruptor's product is worse on the metrics that matter to the incumbent's customers. The disruptor's margins are unattractive. The disruptor's market segment is small. Rational analysis, conducted through the lens of current success, reliably produces the wrong answer.

## Strategic Implication

Recognizing success as the binding constraint suggests that incumbents cannot solve this problem through better analysis alone — they must create organizational structures, resource pools, and evaluation criteria that are deliberately insulated from the logic of the current business. Autonomous units, separate P&Ls, and willingness to cannibalize existing margins are not optional responses to disruption; they are structural requirements for overcoming the dilemma that success creates.
